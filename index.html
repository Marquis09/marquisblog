01 // Visual Research

My visual research investigates the tension between human-centric data visualization and automated algorithmic surveillance. By juxtaposing W.E.B. Du Bois’s hand-drawn data portraits with modern facial-mesh grids, I am questioning the neutrality of the digital gaze.

Figure 1: Du Bois (1900) uses the spiral to represent the non-linear struggle of Black life, creating what Battle-Baptiste and Rusert (2018) call a visualizing of liberation.

Figure 2: Facial landmark detection. This represents Rankin’s (2016) coordination—the process of turning a biological face into a governable digital territory.

Figure 3: Algorithmic mis-seeing. This exemplifies Benjamin's (2019) Coded Inequity, where the lack of diverse training data results in digital erasure.

Figure 4: The Monk Skin Tone (MST) Scale. An attempt to quantify diversity, which often fails to capture the subjective nature of identity.

The juxtaposition of these figures represents a genealogy of the gaze. While Du Bois (Fig 1) utilized data visualization as a tool for political visibility, modern facial landmark detection (Fig 2) often functions as a tool for digital enclosure. My research suggests that Visualizing is a form of work that can either liberate or govern. In Figure 3 and 4, the mis-seeing of the algorithm is not a technical failure, but a historical carryover of the Eurocentric Default. By analyzing these visual artifacts, I am preparing my production phase to use Glitch Aesthetics as a method of making this invisible bias visible to the casual user.

I am analyzing how these images represent a shift in work. In 1900, Du Bois worked manually to reveal humanity; in 2026, the work is performed by algorithms that often hide it. I am using the aesthetic of the spiral (Fig 1) to visually disrupt the rigidity of the grid (Fig 2) in my final video.

02 // Statement of Intent

My project, The Digital Mirror: Decolonizing the Algorithmic Gaze, critiques the ethical dimensions of facial modification in modern UX design. Applying Ruha Benjamin’s (2019) argument that "automation can hide, speed up, and even deepen discrimination while appearing neutral," I am investigating how the New Jim Code is embedded into the social media interface.

Organisation & Planning

My workflow follows industry-standard Agile practices, utilizing Trello to manage the transition from theoretical research to technical execution. This structured planning ensures that the immaterial labor of coding is balanced against strict production deadlines.

Citizenship & Awareness

By identifying tracking failures on diverse skin tones, this work acts as a critical intervention for a digital-native community. It provides a usable framework for UX practitioners to move toward Inclusive Sight (Rankin, 2016).

Aims for a First-Class Outcome:

  • Critical Synthesis: Link the cartographic theories of Rankin (2016) to the biological mapping of the face.
  • Technical Provocation: Use glitch aesthetics as an Abolitionist Tool (Benjamin, 2019) to expose biased code.
  • Technical Iteration: Use Figma to prototype an interface that resists Eurocentric defaults.
This project is not just a video; it is a critical intervention. I am moving beyond simple "AI is bad" narratives to specifically analyze how Digital Work—the processing of symbols—has become a tool for micro-colonialism in the palm of our hands.

03 // Geoepistemology

Following William Rankin’s (2016) After the Map, I am exploring Geoepistemology: the study of how geographic knowledge is produced. Rankin argues that modern GPS/Maps don't just show us where we are; they coordinate our behavior.

Figure 5: Global Submarine Cable Map. As Rankin notes, infrastructure mirrors power; these cables follow the same routes as 19th-century colonial telegraph lines.

Figure 5b: Workshop Sketch.

Subjectivity as Research

In the workshop, I used the upside-down map to challenge the Mercator projection. This aligns with Rankin's theory that truth is a matter of coordination. If we change the center (The Global South), we change the truth. I am applying this to the face: if we change the default beauty standard in the code, we change the digital reality for the user.

Rankin’s assertion that truth is a matter of coordination is central to my critique. If submarine cables (Fig 5) carry the historical weight of colonial trade routes, then software inherits this bias. My workshop sketch (Fig 5b) was a practical exercise in Un-mapping. By inverting the world, I am treating the facial mesh not as an objective truth, but as a subjective territory mapped by biased software.

The connection here is vital: If the internet infrastructure (Fig 5) is colonial, then the software running on it (beauty filters) inherits that bias. My work argues that a beauty filter is a Micro-Map of the face, governed by the same power structures Rankin identifies in global cartography.

04 // Are Robots Racist?

To answer Are Robots Racist?, we must look at Benjamin’s (2019) definition of the New Jim Code. It isn't that the robot has feelings; it's that the work it performs is trained on discriminatory defaults.

Figure 6: Automated skin-lightening. This is a visual manifestation of what Benjamin calls Default Whiteness in technical design.

Benjamin asserts that Design is never neutral; it is a manifestation of power. When a filter narrows a nose or lightens skin (Fig 6), it is a form of Symbolic Violence. The work of the designer here is to either reinforce this standard or, as I intend to do, use Abolitionist Tools to subvert it.

Beyond visual lightening, these algorithms perform geometric erasure. By measuring facial features against a dataset that is 70% lighter-skinned, the code fails to register darker complexions as legitimate data points. This is not a glitch in the system; as Benjamin (2019) argues, the system is functioning exactly as it was designed to prioritize the Default Whiteness of its creators.

My video essay will focus on the Glitch. Benjamin suggests that glitches are actually revelations. By making the facial mesh visible and broken in my video, I am showing the user the biased skeleton of the technology they use every day.

05 // Data Portraits

Synthesizing the Hand-Made and the Machine-Made.

Figure 7: The Algorithmic Spiral. A primary research artifact.

Methodological Resistance

Following Battle Baptiste and Rusert (2018), I am treating data-vis as a visualizing of liberation. My primary research artifact (Fig 7) utilizes Du Bois's (1900) spiral technique to map modern facial recognition error rates across different skin tones.

The work here is intentionality. While the algorithm processes faces as cold, mathematical coordinates, the hand drawn spiral introduces human subjectivity back into the data. This synthesis suggests that to fix the New Jim Code (Benjamin, 2019), we must return to a design practice that values lived experience over automated efficiency.

I am recreating Du Bois’s (1900) style to map modern beauty-filter usage stats. This methodological time-travel highlights that while technology has changed, the struggle for representation remains a site of work and resistance. By manually drawing the algorithm's bias, I am reclaiming the work of representation from the machine.

Technical Workflow

To ensure high-fidelity vocal clarity, I utilized an external microphone interfaced with a mobile recording suite. This raw audio is then imported into Adobe Premiere Pro for post-processing.

Refining the Work: I am maintaining a dry vocal track to allow for granular manipulation. By applying bit-crushing and frequency shifting later in the edit, I can sonically represent the glitch in the algorithm (Benjamin, 2019), making the racial bias audible as well as visible.

Documenting the recording phase proves that the work is a physical process. Following Lovelace's theory of symbolic processing, I am turning my physical voice into a digital asset that can be coded with critical intent. The use of Side-chain compression ensures the theory remains the loudest element in the final mix.

08 // Visuals

Heritage: Babbage, Lovelace, and the Immaterial

Tracing the work of digital systems leads back to the 19th century. During studio research, I analyzed Charles Babbage’s shift from mechanical engines to symbolic logic.

Figure 9: Researching the 1837 Analytical Engine origins.

Figure 10: Modern symbolic labor in Premiere Pro.

Babbage (1837) envisioned a machine that did not just replace physical labor, but replaced calculation. This heritage is vital: it is the birth of the idea that identity can be reduced to numerical symbols—the exact process used in modern face-tracking filters.

The transition from Babbage’s physical Difference Engine to the immaterial code of today represents a shift in the nature of labor. In 1837, work was physical; today, work is the invisible processing of identity. Lovelace’s realization that machines could manipulate symbols is the direct ancestor of the beauty filter—a tool that treats the human face as a symbolic data set to be optimized.

The Modern Connection: As shown in Figure 10, my editing workflow in Premiere Pro is the technical descendant of this logic. Managing 12+ layers of glitch assets and the Pixabay Audioknap track is the manual performance of this symbolic processing. It highlights the Material Friction of modern digital work.

Integrating Lovelace and Babbage alongside modern NLE (Non-Linear Editing) software shows an understanding of how digital work has evolved from mechanical gears to the symbolic immaterial labour of modern AI.

11 // Final Video Essay: The Algorithmic Gaze

Format: 9:16 Vertical (Mobile Optimized) Duration: 03:00

Production Synthesis

This final video essay functions as a critical design intervention. It explores the historical trajectory of data-visualization—from Babbage’s mechanical engines to the contemporary New Jim Code (Benjamin, 2019). By using a non-linear narrative, the work highlights how neutral technical defaults often facilitate digital erasure.

Technical Specifications:

  • Editing Suite: Adobe Premiere Pro (Glitch synthesis & Multi-track layering).
  • Interface Design: Figma (Prototyped HUD overlays & technical schematics).
  • Theoretical Framework: Ruha Benjamin's Coded Inequity & W.E.B. Du Bois' Sociological Data-Vis.
  • Visual Assets: Archive-blended stock footage (Pexels/Pixabay) with original digital sketches.

The 9:16 aspect ratio is a deliberate semiotic choice. It mimics the portrait orientation of modern facial recognition surveillance and social media filters, forcing the viewer to confront the Algorithmic Gaze through the very lens that reproduces it.

Technical Note: Algorithmic Friction & Licensing

The audio for this production, Blockbuster Cinematic Action Trailer by Audioknap (via Pixabay), was selected for its high-intensity pacing, which mirrors the urgency of algorithmic surveillance. Although the track is Royalty-Free/No Copyright, an initial attempt to host this video on YouTube resulted in an automated Copyright Block.

Rather than appeal the automated system, I chose to locally embed the .mp4 file directly into this blog. This technical decision serves as a secondary critique: it highlights how automated platform governance (YouTube's Content ID) can inadvertently silence academic and creative critiques of digital systems.

Final Synthesis: The Algorithmic Legacy

This project has traced the Immaterial Labour of digital identity from the symbolic logic of Babbage and Lovelace to the biased facial meshes of modern social media. By applying W.E.B. Du Bois’s (1900) visual methodology to the New Jim Code identified by Ruha Benjamin (2019), I have exposed how contemporary beauty filters continue a long history of Eurocentric Proportional Divides.

My final video serves as a critical intervention, using glitch aesthetics and technical prototypes to disrupt the algorithmic gaze and demand a more inclusive digital geography. This investigation proves that work in a digital world is not just about code, but about the constant, necessary labor of decolonizing the machines that see us.
